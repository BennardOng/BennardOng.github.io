Stock ASX: "BHP"

Energy stock - MACD Strategy

Will Implementing an algorithm system to match the right market condition with high volatility a viable deployment?

As reviewed from TSLA, growth stocks should be analysed through the potential of the business rather than implementing an algorithm system. What we aim to discover is when market sentiment for commodities are positive and whether volatility during those periods will have better results than a long-term discretionary approach and to solve this, the article found from the RBA written by Ivan Robers, Trent Saunders, Gareth Spence and Natasha Cassidy will provide us clues on our approach. The link is provided below if you are interested in reading the article further.

Article link from RBA


Overview for China's demand

As mentioned above, the article mentions that China's commodity consumption and trade have evolved in step with its pattern of development. The period of interest would be where urbanisation rate rose starting from 2000 to 2014. To narrow down our timeframe, we will use known statistics that can be present and the total overall amount of commodity imports in 2004 Iron ore was 11.7% with a gradual increase to 19.8% by 2014.

Backtest aim

According to the article above, Iron ore had a gradual increase in demand from China, lets not forget that the financial crisis did occur between 2008-2009. This could work in favour for the MACD strategy and be interesting to include since volatility would of been adruptly large and with a higher demand for Iron Ore from China did not stop during these market conditions.

The analysis aims to answer these three approaches:
  • If we invested in the ASX200 from 2004 to 2014 what will be our return?
  • If we invested in "BHP" between 2004 to 2014 what will be our return?
  • If we deployed our MACD algorithm system during favourable market conditions, will it have better returns than the above?
